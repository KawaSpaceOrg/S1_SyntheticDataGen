What is an RF Signal Processing Pipeline?

A radar or communications system must generate a waveform, transmit it, receive a distorted/attenuated copy, and then convert it into digital numbers a computer can process. Each stage has precise mathematics behind it.

🎯 Why Chirp?

A linear frequency-modulated (LFM) chirp sweeps frequency over time, enabling time-frequency analysis and high range-resolution via pulse compression. Used in FMCW radar, sonar, and spread-spectrum comms.

🔢 Why Baseband Simulation?

Simulating the full RF waveform at 10 GHz requires billions of samples per second. Instead, we simulate only the slowly varying baseband envelope (I and Q channels), then up-convert mathematically — orders of magnitude cheaper.

📐 Key Parameters

  • $f_0$ — Start frequency of chirp
  • $B$ — Bandwidth (frequency sweep)
  • $T$ — Pulse duration
  • $k = B/T$ — Chirp rate (Hz/s)
  • $f_c$ — Carrier (RF center) frequency
  • $f_{LO}$ — Local oscillator frequency
  • $f_s$ — Sampling rate (ADC)

📖 Mathematical Convention

We use complex exponential notation throughout. The physical (real) signal is always the real part of the complex representation. This lets us use powerful tools from complex analysis and DSP theory.

$$\text{Physical signal: } x(t) = \Re\{\tilde{x}(t)\}$$
Phase 1

Pulsed LFM Chirp Generation

A pulsed Linear Frequency-Modulated (LFM) chirp is the core radar waveform. The transmitter fires a short chirp burst of duration $T$, stays silent for the rest of the Pulse Repetition Interval (PRI), then repeats — enabling range measurement while controlling average power.

What is a Chirp?

A chirp is a sinusoidal signal whose frequency changes over time. In a linear chirp, the frequency sweeps at a constant rate $k$ called the chirp rate. The instantaneous frequency at time $t$ within a pulse is:

Instantaneous Frequency
$$f_i(t) = f_0 + k \cdot t, \quad k = \frac{B}{T}$$

where $f_0$ is the start frequency, $B$ is the bandwidth, and $T$ is the pulse duration.

Single-Pulse Chirp — Derivation

Phase = integral of instantaneous frequency:

$$\phi(t) = 2\pi \int_0^t f_i(\tau)\, d\tau = 2\pi \left( f_0 t + \frac{k}{2} t^2 \right)$$
$$\boxed{s(t) = \cos\!\left(2\pi f_0 t + \pi k t^2\right), \quad 0 \le t \le T}$$

Complex analytic form: $\;\tilde{s}(t) = e^{j2\pi(f_0 t + \frac{k}{2} t^2)}$

Pulsed LFM — Gating with a Rectangular Window

The pulsed waveform gates the chirp with a rectangular envelope and repeats it every $T_{PRI}$ seconds:

$$s_p(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{N_p - 1} \underbrace{\mathrm{rect}\!\left(\frac{t - nT_{PRI} - T/2}{T}\right)}_{\text{pulse gate}} \cdot \cos\!\left(2\pi f_0(t - nT_{PRI}) + \pi k(t - nT_{PRI})^2\right)$$

where $\mathrm{rect}(u) = 1$ for $|u| \le \tfrac{1}{2}$, else $0$. Two derived quantities:

$$\text{Duty cycle} = \frac{T}{T_{PRI}}, \qquad \text{PRF} = \frac{1}{T_{PRI}}$$

Low duty cycle (≪1) means the receiver is mostly listening, not transmitting — essential for monostatic radar.

📡
💡 Time-Bandwidth Product: $TB = B \cdot T$. Higher TB → better range resolution and more SNR gain from pulse compression. Typical radar: $TB = 100$ to $10{,}000$. The pulsing adds another dimension — duty cycle controls average power vs. peak power.

Complex Baseband Chirp

In simulation we work with the baseband version (centered at DC):

$$s_{BB}(t) = e^{j\pi k t^2}, \quad 0 \le t \le T$$ $$s_{BB}(t) = 0, \quad T < t < T_{PRI}$$

The pulsing is encoded as a simple zero-fill between pulses before up-conversion.

Why Pulsed?

  • Range gating: the delay of the echo tells you $d = c\tau/2$
  • Tx/Rx isolation: transmitter is off while receiver listens
  • Average power control: duty cycle = $T/T_{PRI}$
  • Doppler processing: phase difference across pulses gives velocity
Phase 2

Carrier Wave & RF Modulation

The baseband chirp must be up-converted to a radio frequency carrier before transmission. This is done via IQ (In-phase / Quadrature) modulation.

IQ Modulation — The Core Idea

Any bandpass signal can be written in terms of two baseband signals $I(t)$ and $Q(t)$:

RF Bandpass Signal
$$x_{RF}(t) = I(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) - Q(t)\sin(2\pi f_c t)$$ $$= \Re\!\left\{(I(t) + jQ(t))\,e^{j2\pi f_c t}\right\}$$

For a chirp: $I(t) = \cos(\pi k t^2)$, $Q(t) = \sin(\pi k t^2)$, so $I + jQ = e^{j\pi k t^2}$.

Why multiply by $e^{j2\pi f_c t}$?

Multiplication by $e^{j2\pi f_c t}$ in the time domain is equivalent to a frequency shift of $+f_c$ in the frequency domain:

$$\mathcal{F}\{x_{BB}(t)\cdot e^{j2\pi f_c t}\} = X_{BB}(f - f_c)$$

This moves the baseband spectrum (centered at 0 Hz) up to be centered at the carrier frequency $f_c$.


The real part is taken because physical antennas can only transmit real-valued electromagnetic fields.

I Channel (In-phase)

$I(t) = \cos(\pi k t^2)$ — the real part of the baseband chirp. Modulated onto a cosine carrier $\cos(2\pi f_c t)$.

Q Channel (Quadrature)

$Q(t) = \sin(\pi k t^2)$ — the imaginary part. Modulated onto a sine carrier $-\sin(2\pi f_c t)$. The 90° phase offset gives full vector representation.

📡 RF Envelope: The instantaneous amplitude $A(t) = \sqrt{I(t)^2 + Q(t)^2}$. For a chirp, $A(t) = 1$ (constant). The envelope does not oscillate at the carrier frequency — only the carrier itself does. This is why envelope detection works.
Phase 3

Propagation & Attenuation

As the RF signal travels from transmitter to receivers, it undergoes time delay and amplitude attenuation. Four receivers at different distances receive different copies of the signal.

Free-Space Propagation Model

The voltage amplitude at distance $d$ from the transmitter follows an exponential decay (or inverse-square law in free space):

Amplitude Attenuation
$$A(d) = A_0 \, e^{-\alpha d}$$

$A_0$ = transmit amplitude, $\alpha$ = attenuation coefficient (Np/m or dB/m)

The received signal is also time-delayed by the propagation time $\tau = d/c$:

Received Signal
$$r(t) = A(d)\cdot s(t - \tau), \quad \tau = \frac{d}{c}$$
Free-Space Path Loss (Friis Equation)
$$P_r = P_t \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 G_t G_r$$

In voltage terms: $A(d) \propto 1/d$ for far-field free-space. The exponential model is used for lossy media (atmosphere, cables).

⚠️ Noise in real systems: Each receiver also picks up thermal noise $n(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. The signal-to-noise ratio degrades with distance: $\text{SNR}(d) \propto A(d)^2 / \sigma^2$. Pulse compression (matched filtering) recovers $\approx TB$ dB of SNR.
Phase 4

Downconversion — Mixing to Baseband

The received RF signal must be shifted back down from the carrier frequency to baseband (near DC) for efficient digital processing. This is done by mixing with a Local Oscillator (LO).

The Mixing Operation

A mixer multiplies the incoming RF signal by the LO reference. Using the product-to-sum identity:

Mixing Identity
$$\cos(2\pi f_{RF} t)\cdot\cos(2\pi f_{LO} t) = \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi(f_{RF}-f_{LO})t) + \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi(f_{RF}+f_{LO})t)$$

This produces two output components: a difference (IF) at $f_{RF} - f_{LO}$ and a sum at $f_{RF} + f_{LO}$.

Full Downconversion Chain
$$x_{RF}(t) = \Re\{x_{BB}(t)\, e^{j2\pi f_c t}\}$$ $$\downarrow \text{ multiply by } e^{-j2\pi f_{LO} t} \text{ (complex demodulation)}$$ $$x_{IF}(t) = x_{BB}(t)\, e^{j2\pi(f_c - f_{LO})t}$$ $$\downarrow \text{ if } f_{LO} = f_c \text{ (direct conversion / homodyne)}$$ $$\boxed{x_{IF}(t) = x_{BB}(t) = I(t) + jQ(t)}$$

Low-pass Filter (LPF)

After mixing, a low-pass filter removes the sum component at $f_{RF} + f_{LO}$. Only the difference component at $|f_{RF} - f_{LO}|$ passes through. This is the Intermediate Frequency (IF) signal, or baseband if $f_{LO} = f_{RF}$.

Homodyne vs Heterodyne

  • Homodyne: $f_{LO} = f_c$ → direct to baseband in one step.
  • Heterodyne: $f_{LO} \ne f_c$ → output is at IF $= f_c - f_{LO}$, then filtered again.

Modern software-defined radios mostly use homodyne (direct conversion) with IQ demodulation.

💡 IQ Demodulation: The real mixer gives only $I(t)$. To recover $Q(t)$, a second mixer with a 90°-shifted LO ($\sin(2\pi f_{LO}t)$) is used. Together they recover the full complex baseband $x_{BB}(t) = I(t) + jQ(t)$.
Phase 5

Baseband Equivalent Signal (IQ Representation)

The most important concept in modern RF simulation: represent the entire RF waveform using just two slowly-varying signals I and Q, without ever explicitly computing the high-frequency carrier.

The Baseband Equivalent Signal

Any bandpass signal $x(t)$ with bandwidth $B \ll f_c$ can be written as:

Bandpass Signal Decomposition
$$x(t) = I(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) - Q(t)\sin(2\pi f_c t)$$

The complex baseband equivalent is:

Baseband Equivalent (Analytic Signal)
$$x_{BB}(t) = I(t) + jQ(t) \equiv A(t)\, e^{j\phi(t)}$$

where $A(t) = \sqrt{I^2+Q^2}$ is the envelope and $\phi(t) = \arctan(Q/I)$ is the phase.

Why This Works — Sampling Rate Comparison

By the Nyquist theorem, to sample an RF signal at $f_c = 10\,\text{GHz}$ with bandwidth $B = 100\,\text{MHz}$:

$$f_{s,RF} \ge 2f_c = 20\,\text{GHz} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad 2\times10^{10} \text{ samples/sec}$$

But the baseband equivalent only needs:

$$f_{s,BB} \ge 2B = 200\,\text{MHz} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad 2\times10^8 \text{ samples/sec}$$

Savings factor: $f_c / B = 100\times$ fewer samples!

I Channel (In-phase)

$$I(t) = \cos\!\left(\pi k t^2\right)$$

The projection of the phasor onto the real axis. Slowly oscillating compared to the carrier.

Q Channel (Quadrature)

$$Q(t) = \sin\!\left(\pi k t^2\right)$$

The projection onto the imaginary axis. 90° phase-shifted from I. Together, I+jQ traces a unit circle in the complex plane.

✅ The phasor trajectory: For a chirp, $|x_{BB}(t)| = 1$ always. The phasor rotates at the instantaneous baseband frequency $f_i(t) = kt$, which is why you see an accelerating spiral in the phasor plot.
Phase 6

Analog-to-Digital Conversion (ADC)

The final step converts the continuous-time analog baseband signal into discrete-time digital samples that a DSP processor or FPGA can process.

What is an ADC?

An Analog-to-Digital Converter samples an analog waveform at a fixed sampling rate $f_s = 1/T_s$ and quantizes each sample to a finite number of bits. The output is a sequence of numbers:

Sampling Theorem
$$x[n] = x(t)\big|_{t = nT_s} = x(nT_s), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

Nyquist criterion: $f_s \ge 2B_{\max}$ to avoid aliasing.

Quantization — Bit Depth
$$\Delta = \frac{V_{FS}}{2^N}, \quad x_q[n] = \Delta \cdot \text{round}\!\left(\frac{x[n]}{\Delta}\right)$$

$N$ = number of bits, $V_{FS}$ = full-scale voltage range, $\Delta$ = quantization step. Quantization noise power: $\sigma_q^2 \approx \Delta^2/12$.


Dynamic range (SQNR) of an $N$-bit ADC:

$$\text{SQNR} \approx 6.02N + 1.76 \text{ dB}$$
⚠️ Aliasing: If $f_s < 2 f_{signal}$, the sampled signal appears at the wrong frequency: $f_{alias} = |f_{signal} - \text{round}(f_{signal}/f_s)\cdot f_s|$. Try setting $f_{signal}$ above $f_s / 2$ in the slider above to see this effect.
Summary

End-to-End Pipeline Summary

Putting it all together — the complete RF signal chain from waveform generation to digital samples.

Stage Operation Key Equation Domain
1. Chirp Gen Generate LFM waveform $s(t) = e^{j\pi k t^2}$ Baseband, continuous
2. RF Modulation Up-convert to carrier $f_c$ $x_{RF}(t) = \Re\{s(t)\,e^{j2\pi f_c t}\}$ RF bandpass, continuous
3. Propagation Delay + attenuation $r(t) = A_0 e^{-\alpha d}\,s(t - d/c)$ RF bandpass, continuous
4. Downconversion Mix with LO, LPF $y(t) = r(t)\cdot e^{-j2\pi f_c t}$ Baseband, continuous
5. Baseband IQ Extract I, Q channels $x_{BB}(t) = I(t) + jQ(t)$ Baseband, continuous
6. ADC Sample & quantize $x[n] = x_{BB}(nT_s)$ Baseband, discrete
End-to-End (Simulation shortcut)

In simulation, we skip stages 2–4 entirely by noting that upconversion + propagation + downconversion (with identical $f_c$) reduces to:

$$r_{BB}(t) = A_0 e^{-\alpha d}\, x_{BB}(t - \tau)\, e^{-j2\pi f_c \tau}$$

The term $e^{-j2\pi f_c \tau}$ is a constant phase shift due to the RF carrier propagating over distance $d = c\tau$. This is the core of every radar simulator.

✅ Key Takeaways

  • Chirp gives high range resolution via large bandwidth $B$
  • IQ modulation encodes all signal info in two real signals
  • Propagation adds delay and attenuation — both distance-dependent
  • Mixing + LPF moves the signal from RF to baseband
  • Baseband simulation saves $f_c / B$ compute factor
  • ADC converts to discrete samples; Nyquist prevents aliasing
